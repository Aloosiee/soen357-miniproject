MedCompanion

UX/UI case study — designing a mobile health super app for Canadians managing chronic conditions, medications, and healthcare appointments.

Elias Haddad — 40196175

Problem Statement

The MedCompanion application was created for Canadians who live with chronic diseases such as hypertension and type 2 diabetes. This application allows users to manage their drug-seeking behaviors and communicate with their primary care providers from within one piece of software.

Non-adherence to medications is a huge problem in the Canadian health care sector. According to Think Research Canada (2023) [1], as many as 20-30% of prescriptions written in Canada are never filled and 26% of individuals who do fill their prescriptions report taking them differently than prescribed. A CMAJ article [2] has reported that about 5% of Canadians aged 12 and over report cost-related non-adherence to their prescribed medications, and people with one or more long-term health problems are at least 3.4 times more likely to experience medication non-adherence than people without chronic health problems. Approximately 23% of Canadians have trouble remembering to take their medications on time [1].

This UX report provides a summary of how we approached the development of our product, including our user research, the development of user personas, mapping user journeys through the application, creating wireframes, developing a click-through prototype, and creating a usability testing plan.

User Research

For research purposes, three types of secondary data were utilized to define the user population for the target application: (1) Academic Research Studies: PubMed, JMIR, and MDPI all have a large repository of peer-reviewed academic research (2016-2024) related to barriers that prevent patients from taking their medications, how well the mHealth apps are used by patients, and how to manage chronic illnesses in Canada; (2) Canadian National Surveys: Statistics Canada, Canadian Community Health Survey (CCHS), Think Research Canada, and HEC Montreal partnered to conduct a cross-sectional study of mHealth use by 4,109 Canadians; and (3) Current UX Studies: Two current examples are a Czech Republic pilot study (2024) that evaluated a medication adherence app for patients with Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) and congestive heart failure using ISO 14915 usability specifications [4] and a survey conducted by the Canadian Journal of Hospital Pharmacy in 2023 to determine how older adults were utilizing technology at Saint John Regional Hospital in New Brunswick [5].

2.2.1 Medication Non-Adherence in Canada

A Canadian Family Physician study of over 91,000 prescriptions from Manitoba primary care patients found that non-adherence rates for antihypertensives reached 30.3% — among the highest of any drug class studied [6]. This is clinically significant given that hypertension is one of the most common chronic conditions managed in Canadian primary care. A Statistics Canada analysis of the Canadian Community Health Survey found that Canadians with chronic conditions who spent 5% or more of household income on out-of-pocket drug costs were at substantially elevated risk of cost-related non-adherence [7].

A systematic review of 26 Canadian studies (n = 483,065 participants) published in Systematic Reviews found cost-related non-adherence rates ranging from 5.1% to 10.2% of the general population, with predictors including low income, lack of drug insurance, and presence of chronic illness [8]. Forgetfulness and routine disruption are also well-documented barriers: a qualitative systematic review of 16 studies and 403 patients found that medication is most commonly missed when patients are away from their regular routine [9].

2.2.2 Technology Adoption Among Canadian Older Adults

An HEC Montréal / Canada Health Infoway national cross-sectional survey conducted on 4,109 Canadian residents shows that 47.4% of older adults aged 65 and over said they use a smartphone, but only 39% have shared mHealth data with their health care providers, highlighting the large gap between access to technology and how it can be effectively used for health purposes [3]. Another national study of 2,000 Canadian Seniors found that 85.2% own a computer, 82.6% use the Internet regularly, however, there was still a low utilization of eHealth applications for appointment scheduling, access to patient portals, and connecting to health care providers [10]. A survey published by The Canadian Journal of Hospital Pharmacy looking at older adults aged 60+ who were surveyed through Saint John Regional Hospital located in New Brunswick determined the following: older adults are very interested in using medication reminders and appointment management apps, however, their main barriers to adoption are perceived complexity, privacy issues, and lack of confidence [5].

2.2.3 mHealth Usability for Older Adults

A systematic review published in JMIR mHealth found that excessive navigation depth, small text size, absence of visual aids, and too many simultaneous choices are the most common usability problems in health-related applications. The main recommendation was to keep all essential elements within two taps of each other and to provide visual illustrations of text or pictograms [11].

In a newly conducted pilot study from 2024, researchers in the Czech Republic evaluated a "real world" medication management application using ISO 14915 coding standards on patients with chronic diseases taking medication. Three significant findings from this study are relevant in designing a MedCompanion: patients preferred a colour-coded interface over a monochrome interface; pictograms helped reduce the number of times the patient had to read the sequence of events associated with taking their medications; and using confirmation dialogues after patients completed a dose entry helped reduce accidental entries when taking medications [4].

2.2.4 Polypharmacy

Polypharmacy (the concurrent use of five or more medications) is becoming more commonplace for Canadians over the age of 65 as many of them suffer from multiple chronic illnesses. In a study of a large British Columbia administrative health database (n=3,906,377 adults), researchers discovered that individuals' adherence to the use of their prescribed medication was disrupted by external stressors (e.g., employment or relationship issues) and routine changes (e.g., vacation or medical appointments), which underscore the need for the development of adaptive reminder systems [12].

Sketches & Wireframes

5.1 Information Architecture

MedCompanion uses a flat five-tab navigation structure: Home, Medications, Appointments, Messages, and Reports. This architecture keeps every core feature accessible within two taps, directly addressing the JMIR 2023 systematic review's recommendation to minimise navigation depth for older adults [11]. Initial paper sketches explored a deeper hierarchical structure, but early iteration showed that placing critical actions more than two levels deep increased cognitive load and error rates.

Home Dashboard

The home screen displays today's medications with large TAKE / TAKEN buttons and an adherence streak counter. The next upcoming appointment appears below the medication list. All content is above the fold to avoid scrolling. Design rationale: a 2024 Czech usability study found that the first interaction with a health app is critical to adoption — the interface must be immediately comprehensible and uncluttered [4].

Add Medication

Users can photograph a pill bottle; OCR extracts the medication name and dose. The flow is divided into four steps (scan, confirm details, set reminder, choose pill photo), one action per screen. This reduces typing burden — a documented usability barrier for older adults — and allows users to visually identify their medications by photo, addressing a noted concern among patients managing multiple drugs [11].

Medication Reminder

Full-screen reminder showing the pill photo, medication name, and dose. Three buttons: TAKEN, Remind me in 30 minutes, and Skip. A green confirmation screen with an undo button will appear for three seconds after selecting TAKEN. The confirmation pattern was developed using the recommendations from the 2024 Czech pilot study, which confirmed that the confirmation pattern can prevent accidental marking of doses in real patient trials [4].

Appointments

Listing of appointments. All appointments show future appointments, and the type (in-person/telehealth), date, time and provider. A one-tap Join button appears 15 minutes before a telehealth visit. Appointment reminders at 24 hours and 1 hour are enabled by default.

Reports

A heatmap calendar displays daily adherence for the current month; green indicates full adherence, yellow indicates a delayed dose, and red indicates a missed dose. A monthly bar chart shows per-medication adherence percentages. A one-tap PDF export allows users to share adherence data with their physician at their next visit.

Prototype

The creation of a low-to-medium fidelity prototype in Figma involved clickable navigation for demonstration purposes without any functional use. This prototype encompasses five distinct user flows as follows: FLOWS A - onboarding, adding medication via scanning the bottle, setting a reminder and returning back to the home dashboard; FLOWS B - navigating to home screen, receiving notification from system, clicking on TAKEN, seeing confirmation screen that it's green (taken) and then returning back to home screen after clicking back key; FLOWS C/D - adjustments based on current time zone (Travel Mode for David), adjusting reminder/time to allow for time zone change and returning the adjusted schedule each time an adjustment is made; FLOWS E - sending a new message (Doctor) via text message tab, sending message, receiving a confirmation it has been delivered.

All visual design decisions are based on user research, including the application of a minimum of 44×44 pt touch targets on all interactive elements according to WCAG 2.1, and JMIR 2023's recommendation [11]; all text within the app has a WCAG AA color contrast (4.5:1); confirmation dialogs will be presented for all actions that cannot be undone, based on recommendations from the Czech usability study [4].

Usability Testing Plan

7.1 Goals

The usability test aims to answer four questions: (1) Can users complete core medication tasks without assistance? (2) Where do errors and confusion occur? (3) How trustworthy and easy to use do participants perceive the app? (4) What is the System Usability Scale (SUS) score for the prototype?

7.2 Participants

Nielsen's finding that five users reveal approximately 85% of usability issues informs the target recruitment of six to eight participants [13]:

  • 👩‍🦳
    3–4 participants aged 60+ with at least one chronic condition and limited mHealth experience (Margaret archetype)
  • 👨🏾‍💼
    2–3 participants aged 35–55 with a chronic condition and high technology comfort (David archetype)
  • 📱
    All must own a smartphone and must not have previously seen the prototype

7.4 Method and Analysis

Each session runs 45–60 minutes and is conducted remotely via screenshare or in person. The think-aloud protocol is used throughout: participants verbalise their reasoning as they navigate. After all tasks, participants complete the 10-item SUS questionnaire. The approach follows best practices identified in a 2025 systematic review of usability testing in 68 mHealth studies [13].

Qualitative observations are grouped thematically using affinity diagramming (e.g., "navigation confusion," "notification preference," "text size concerns"). Issues are prioritised by a severity-frequency matrix; the most blocking and most frequently encountered problems are addressed first in the next design iteration.

Reflection

The use of published literature — especially research conducted in Canada — for grounding design decisions helped to differentiate features that add value for users and those that can be added easily but are unlikely to be used. For example, the travel-mode time zone adjustment became a core feature rather than a secondary feature since research shows routine disruption is one of the primary reasons that users miss taking their medications [9].

The single most important issue regarding design was trying to meet the needs of two different types of users who have different, and sometimes conflicting, requirements for an application. For instance, what provides the least friction for Margaret (i.e., guided flows, large objects, minimal choices) may be perceived by David as being too limiting (i.e., David wants analytics, speed, as well as configurability). One method of resolving this issue was through progressive disclosure; for example, while the home screen is intentionally kept minimalistic, users who are more experienced can access more advanced features (i.e., analytics, calendar sync, telehealth) at any time, but they are not visually or functionally forced on the less-experienced user.

Finally, the journey maps helped us to identify emotional pain points that might not be obvious based on adherence data alone; for instance, adding an expected time to respond to a message in the user interface was implemented due to Margaret's anxiety regarding how long it would take for her to receive a response from her doctor.

Because this method develops a persona based on population level research data collected from a wide variety of different types of individual interviews with real live users, you cannot test how users interact with the product in person through a traditional usability test where only five- to eight people participate. The purpose of the next phase of this study is to create a caregiver-facing view; one of the biggest predictors of older adults' adherence is whether their family members are involved in their health care. You also need to create separate logging modules for use with patients who have diabetes, high blood pressure, etc.
